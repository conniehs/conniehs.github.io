Basic Google Search

Getting Started

Google is the most widely used search engine, processing billions of searches daily. To get the best results, you need to understand how to construct effective search queries.

Basic Search Tips:

  • Use specific keywords for your topic
  • Start with simple terms and then add details
  • Skip common words like "the" or "and"
  • Ignore perfect spelling - Google will suggest corrections

Using Quotation Marks [" "]

Exact Phrase Searching

When you put words in quotation marks, Google searches for that exact phrase in that exact order.

When to use quotation marks:

  • Searching for a specific phrase or title
  • Looking for a famous quote
  • Finding a specific name or term
  • When word order matters
Example:

Without quotes: climate change effects

Results include pages with "climate," "change," and "effects" anywhere.

With quotes: "climate change effects"

Results only include pages with this exact phrase.

Using the Minus Sign [-]

Excluding Unwanted Results

Use the minus sign (-) to exclude specific words from your search results. This helps eliminate irrelevant information.

How to use the minus sign:

  • Put the minus sign directly before the word (no space)
  • Use it to exclude common but irrelevant terms
  • Help narrow down broad topics
  • Remove commercial results when seeking information
Example:

Problem: Searching "apple" returns mostly results about the technology company

Solution: "apple -computer -iphone -technology"

This excludes Apple Inc. results and focuses on the fruit.

Using OR [OR]

Searching for Alternative Terms

Use OR (in capital letters) to search for one term or another. This is useful when you want results that include any of several related terms.

When to use OR:

  • When you have synonyms for your topic
  • To broaden your search results
  • When different terms might be used for the same concept
  • To find comprehensive information on related topics
Example:

Search: "vacation OR holiday destinations"

Results will include pages with either "vacation" or "holiday" destinations.

Search: "automobile OR car OR vehicle maintenance"

Results will include pages with any of the terms "automobile," "car," or "vehicle maintenance"‚Äîcovering all common terms for motor vehicles.

Site-Specific Searches [site:]

Searching Within Specific Websites

Use "site:" followed by a website domain to search only within that specific site. This is particularly useful for finding information on large websites or specific types of sources.

Useful site-specific searches:

  • site:edu - Search only educational institutions
  • site:gov - Search only government websites
  • site:org - Search only organisation websites
  • site:wikipedia.org - Search only Wikipedia
Example:

Search: "renewable energy site:edu"

Results will include pages about renewable energy exclusively from educational institutions.

Search: "climate data site:gov"

Results will include government pages providing climate data.

File Type Searches [filetype:]

Finding Specific Document Types

Evaluating Sources

Determining Source Credibility

Not all information online is reliable. You need to evaluate sources to ensure the information you use is credible and accurate.

Key factors to consider:

  • Authority: Who wrote or published the information?
  • Currency: How recent is the information?
  • Accuracy: Are there sources cited? Can facts be verified?
  • Purpose: Why was this information created?
  • Objectivity: Is the information biased or balanced?

Checking Multiple Sources

Cross-Referencing Information

Always verify information by checking multiple sources. This helps ensure accuracy and provides a more complete understanding of your topic.

Best practices for multiple sources:

  • Check at least 2-3 different sources
  • Look for sources that cite the same original research
  • Compare different perspectives on controversial topics
  • Use a variety of source types (academic, news, government)
  • Note any contradictions and investigate further
Source Diversity Example:

Topic: Effects of sleep deprivation

  • Academic source: University sleep study
  • Medical source: Centre for Health Protection (Department of Health, HKSAR)
  • Research source: HKU Sleep Research Clinic and Laboratory
  • News source: Recent research coverage

Understanding Search Results

Reading Search Result Pages

Google search results contain valuable information beyond just the link. Learning to read result pages effectively helps you choose the best sources quickly.

Key elements of search results:

  • Title (blue link): The clickable headline of the page
  • URL (green text): The web address showing the source
  • Snippet: Brief description or excerpt from the page
  • Date: When the page was published or last updated
  • Site links: Additional links to sections of the website
Reading the URL:

.edu = Educational institution (universities, colleges)

.gov = Government website

.org = Organisation (often non-profit)

.com = Commercial website

Country codes: .uk, .ca, .au indicate the country of origin

Advanced Search Combinations

Combining Search Techniques

For complex research topics, you can combine multiple search techniques to create powerful, precise queries.

Combination strategies:

  • Use quotation marks with site-specific searches
  • Combine OR with minus signs to include some terms while excluding others
  • Use file type searches with specific sites
  • Add date ranges for current information
Advanced Search Examples:

"renewable energy" site:edu filetype:pdf

Results will include PDF documents about renewable energy from educational institutions.

vaccination OR immunisation site:gov -commercial

Results will include government pages about vaccination or immunisation, excluding commercial sites.

Google Scholar

Academic and Scholarly Research

Google Scholar is a specialised search engine that focuses on scholarly literature, including articles, theses, books, conference papers, and citations from academic publishers.

Benefits of Google Scholar:

  • Access to peer-reviewed academic papers
  • Citation tracking and related articles
  • Author profile information
  • Advanced search options for academic research
  • Links to full-text articles when available
When to Use Google Scholar:
  • Writing research papers or academic assignments
  • Looking for peer-reviewed studies
  • Finding original research on scientific topics
  • Checking citation counts for source credibility
  • Accessing academic books and conference proceedings

Understanding AI-Generated Answers

Google's AI Overview Feature

Google now sometimes displays AI-generated answers at the top of search results. These are created by artificial intelligence and provide quick summaries, but they require careful evaluation.

Key characteristics of AI-generated answers:

  • Appear in a special box at the top of results
  • Usually labelled as "AI Overview" or similar
  • Synthesise information from multiple sources
  • May include source links for verification
  • Can sometimes contain errors or oversimplifications
Example AI-Generated Answer:

Search: "How does photosynthesis work?"

AI Overview might show: "Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water into glucose and oxygen. This occurs primarily in the chloroplasts of plant cells using chlorophyll. The process involves two main stages: light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle."

This would be followed by source links to verify the information.

How to Use AI Answers Responsibly

  • Verify information: Always check the provided source links
  • Cross-reference: Compare with other reliable sources
  • Use as a starting point: Not as your final or only source
  • Be aware of limitations: AI can make mistakes or miss nuances
  • Check for recent information: AI summaries may not include the latest developments

Paraphrasing and Summarising Research

Putting Information in Your Own Words

Once you find reliable information, you need to paraphrase it effectively. Paraphrasing means expressing someone else's ideas using your own words and sentence structure while maintaining the original meaning.

Why paraphrase?

  • Demonstrates your understanding of the material
  • Avoids plagiarism while using source information
  • Helps integrate sources smoothly into your writing
  • Shows critical thinking and analysis

Effective Paraphrasing Techniques

  1. Read and understand: Make sure you fully comprehend the original text
  2. Identify key ideas: Focus on the main concepts, not minor details
  3. Use different words: Replace vocabulary with synonyms where appropriate
  4. Change sentence structure: Reorganise how the information is presented
  5. Maintain meaning: Ensure your version conveys the same message
  6. Cite the source: Always give credit to the original author
Paraphrasing Example:

Original: "Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns, primarily caused by human activities such as burning fossil fuels."

Good Paraphrase: "Human actions, particularly the combustion of fossil fuels, are the main drivers of climate change, which involves lasting changes in worldwide temperature and weather systems."

Why this is a good paraphrase:

  • Uses different vocabulary ("combustion" instead of "burning", "drivers" instead of "caused by")
  • Changes sentence structure (starts with "Human actions" instead of "Climate change")
  • Preserves the original meaning completely
  • Demonstrates understanding by reorganising the information logically

Poor Paraphrase: "Climate change means long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns, mainly caused by human activities like burning fossil fuels." (Too similar to original - only minor word changes)

Additional Tips for Academic Writing

  • Use transition phrases: "According to [source]," "Research indicates," "The study found that"
  • Combine information: Merge ideas from multiple sources into cohesive paragraphs
  • Add your analysis: Include your own interpretation or connection to your thesis
  • Vary your approach: Mix paraphrasing with direct quotes and your own ideas
  • Keep track of sources: Maintain clear records for proper citation
Remember: Even when paraphrasing, you must cite your sources. Paraphrasing without citation is still plagiarism. Always give credit to the original authors of the ideas you're using.
